# 2D Vectors
We must first introduce the concept of a vector. We call $x$ for the vector's 1st coordinate and $y$ for the vector's 2nd coordinate. The coordinates of the vector are written as $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \end{array}\right) $$
Furthermore, for 2 vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$, $$ \vec{a} \| \vec{b} \quad \leftrightarrow \quad \vec{b}=k \cdot \vec{a} \quad, \quad k \neq 0 $$ where $\vec{a} \| \vec{b}$ means that the 2 vectors are parallel. One can also define a vector as the line between 2 points as follows: Let $A=\left(a_1, a_2\right)$ and $B=\left(b_1, b_2\right)$ be 2 items in the plane. The vector between $A$ and $B$ is given by $$ \overrightarrow{A B}=\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1-a_1 \\ b_2-a_2 \end{array}\right) $$ ![[Pasted image 20230825150521.png]]
## Operations
Suppose that $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad \vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{array}\right) $$ Then we can perform these operations:
$$ \begin{array}{r} \vec{a}+\vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1+b_1 \\ a_2+b_2 \end{array}\right) \\ \vec{a}-\vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1-b_1 \\ a_2-b_2 \end{array}\right) \\ k \vec{a}=k\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} k a_1 \\ k a_2 \end{array}\right) \\ k(\vec{a}+\vec{b})=k \vec{a}+k \vec{b}\\ |\vec{a}|=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2} \end{array} $$

## Unit vector
If we have a vector $\vec{a}$ with a distance $|\vec{a}|$ then we can find the unit vector $\overrightarrow{e_a}$ with the same direction. A unit vector has the property that $|\vec{e}|=1$. If we set aside a unit vector $\vec{e}$ starting in $\mathrm{O}$, the endpoint will lie on the unit circle. Given a vector $\vec{a}$, one can find the unit vector with the same direction with the following calculation. $$ \overrightarrow{e_a}=\frac{1}{|\vec{a}|} \vec{a} $$
## Dot product
The *scalar product* or *dot product* $$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} $$ of two actual vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ is defined by $$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}=|\vec{a} \| \vec{b}| \cos (v) $$ where $v$ is the radian angle between $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$. Further, we put $$ \vec{a} \cdot \overrightarrow{0}=\overrightarrow{0} \cdot \vec{a}=0 $$ for all vectors $\vec{a}$.

The dot product between 2 vectors is $$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}=a_1 b_1+a_2 b_2 $$ and the arc length between 2 vectors can be found by $$ \frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|} =\frac{a_1 b_1+a_2 b_2}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|} =\cos (v) $$

Given three vectors, $\vec{a}, \vec{b}$ and $\vec{c}$ , these rules apply:
$$ \begin{aligned} \vec{a}^2 & =\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a}=|\vec{a}|^2 \\ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} & =\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} \\ \vec{a} \cdot(\vec{b}+\vec{c}) & =\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}+\vec{a} \cdot \vec{c} \\ \vec{a} \cdot(k \vec{b}) & =k(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}) \end{aligned} $$
## Orthogonality
Given 2 vectors $\vec{a} \neq \overrightarrow{0}$ and $\vec{b} \neq \overrightarrow{0}$. If $$ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}=0 $$ Then the angle between the 2 vectors is $\frac{\pi}{2}$. If this applies, then we call the vectors orthogonal: $$ \vec{a} \perp \vec{b} $$
## Cross vector
An important vector that is always orthogonal on a vector $\vec{a}$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$, is the cross vector. If $\vec{a}$ is a vector with coordinates $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{array}\right)$$ We define the cross vector $\widehat{\vec{a}}$ by $$ \widehat{\vec{a}}=\left(\begin{array}{c} -a_2\\ a_1 \end{array}\right) $$
![[Pasted image 20230825150634.png]]
Given $\vec{a}$ then we have that $$ \vec{a} \perp \widehat{\vec{a}} $$ Furthermore, we have the following calculation rules for the cross vector: $$ \begin{gathered} \widehat{t \vec{a}}=t \cdot \widehat{\vec{a}} \\ \widehat{\vec{a}+\vec{b}}=\widehat{\vec{a}}+\widehat{\vec{b}} \\ |\widehat{\vec{a}}|=|\vec{a}| \end{gathered} $$
## Determinant
We define $$ \operatorname{det}(\vec{a}, \vec{b})=\widehat{\vec{a}} \cdot \vec{b} $$ for the determinant between $\vec{a}, \vec{b}$.

We usually calculate this expression by inserting the coordinate of the vectors a $2 \times 2$ scheme with vertical sides $$ \operatorname{det}(\vec{a}, \vec{b})=|\vec{a} \vec{b}|=\left|\begin{array}{cc} a_1 &b_1 \\ a_2 &b_2 \end{array}\right|=a_1 b_2-a_2 b_1 $$![[Pasted image 20230825150845.png]] 
Given two vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$. Then we have to $$ \begin{gathered} \operatorname{det}(\vec{b}, \vec{a})=-\operatorname{det}(\vec{a}, \vec{b}) \\ \operatorname{det}(\vec{a}, \vec{b})=0 \Leftrightarrow \vec{a} \| \vec{b} \\ |\operatorname{det}(\vec{a}, \vec{b})|=\text { area of the parallelogram stretched by } \vec{a} \text { and } \vec{b} \end{gathered} $$

## Projection
![[Pasted image 20230826082642.png]]
Suppose $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ are vectors. Then the *projection* $\overrightarrow{b_a}$ of $\vec{b}$ of $\vec{a}$ is given by:$$ \overrightarrow{b_a}=\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}|^2} \vec{a} $$
As:
$$ \left|\overrightarrow{b_a}\right|=\left|\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}|^2} \vec{a}\right|=\frac{|\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}|} {|\vec{a}|^2}|\vec{a}|=\frac{|\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}|} {|\vec{a}|} $$ we can calculate the *length* of the projection of $\vec{b}$ on $\vec{a}$ by the following formula. $$ \left|\overrightarrow{b_a}\right|=\frac{|\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}|} {|\vec{a}|} $$

# Lines in the plane
## Direction vectors
If we're given a line in the plane, we call a vector that is parallel to the line a *direction vector* for the line. There are infinitely many vectors that are parallel to the line, so a line has infinitely many direction vectors. In addition, all parallel lines have the same direction vectors. We can describe a straight line l completely from a known point $P_0$ and a direction vector $\vec{r}$. If $P$ is any second point of 1, then $\overrightarrow{P_0 P}$ must be parallel to $\vec{r}$, so there must be a real number $t$, such that:
$$
\overrightarrow{P_0 P}=t \vec{r}
$$
![[Pasted image 20230826084921.png]]
The line $l$ through $P_0=\left(x_0, y_0\right)$ with directional vector $\vec{r}=\left(\begin{array}{l}r_1\\r_2\end{array}\right)$ has the parametric representation :$$ \left(\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} x_0 \\ y_0 \end{array}\right)+t\left(\begin{array}{l} r_1 \\ r_2 \end{array}\right), \quad \text { where } t \in \mathbb{R} $$
## Normal vectors
If a vector is orthogonal to a line we call it a *normal vector* to that line.

The line through the point $P_0\left(x_0, y_0\right)$ with normal vector $$ \vec{n}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a \\ b \end{array}\right) $$ has the equation $$ a\left(x-x_0\right)+b\left(y-y_0\right)=0 $$ which we can write in the form $$ a x+b y+c=0 $$ where $c=-a x_0-b y_0$

## Slope of orthogonal  lines
Suppose lines $l$ and $m$ have slope coefficients $a_l$ $a_m$, respectively. Then it applies that: $$ l \perp m \quad \leftrightarrow \quad a_l \cdot a_m=-1 $$
## Distance from point to line
The distance from $P\left(x_1, y_1\right)$ to the line $l$ with equation $a x+b y+c=0$ is given by $$ \operatorname{dist}(P, l)=\frac{\left|a x_1+b y_1+c\right|} {\sqrt{a^2+b^2}} $$![[Pasted image 20230826090915.png]]

# Circle equation
The standard form equation of a circle consists of points $P$ in the plane that all lie the same distance $r$, the radius, from a point $C$, the centre of the circle.
![[Pasted image 20230827134421.png]]
A point $P(x, y)$ lies on the circle with the centre in $C(a, b)$ and radius $r$, if and only if $$ (x-a)^2+(y-b)^2=r^2 $$
# 3D vectors
We will now look at vectors in 3 dimensions. A vector $\vec{a}$ is noted as follows $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right) $$ where $a_1$ is the first coordinate, etc. The following calculation operations apply to vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Suppose that $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right) $$ Then the length of $\vec{a}$ is given by $$ |\vec{a}|=\sqrt{a_1^2+a_2^2+a_3^2} $$ Suppose that $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad \vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{array}\right) $$ Then these rules apply: $$ \begin{aligned} \vec{a}+\vec{b} & =\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right)+\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1+b_1 \\ a_2+b_2 \\ a_3+b_3 \end{array}\right) \\ \vec{a}-\vec{b} & =\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1-b_1 \\ a_2-b_2 \\ a_3-b_3 \end{array}\right) \\ s \vec{a} & =s\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} s a_1 \\ s a_2 \\ s a_3 \end{array}\right) \end{aligned} $$
Having given two points $A\left(a_1, a_2, a_3\right)$ and $B\left(b_1, b_2, b_3\right)$, then we can find the coordinates of $\overrightarrow{A B}$ at $$ \begin{aligned} \overrightarrow{A B} & =\overrightarrow{A O}+\overrightarrow{O B} \\ & =-\overrightarrow{O A}+\overrightarrow{O B} \\ & =\overrightarrow{O B}-\overrightarrow{O A} \\ & =\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{array}\right)-\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right) \\ & =\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1-a_1 \\ b_2-a_2 \\ b_3-a_3 \end{array}\right) \end{aligned} $$ Many of the calculation rules that we know for vectors in the plan, we can extend to space.
## Dot product
$$ \begin{gathered} \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right) \quad \text { and } \quad \vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{array}\right) \\ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}=a_1 b_1+a_2 b_2+a_3 b_3 \end{gathered} $$ Rules of calculation for the dot product for vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ If $\vec{a}, \vec{b}$, and $\vec{c}$ are vectors in space, and $s$ is a number, then the following arithmetic rules apply $$ \begin{aligned} \vec{a}^2 & =\vec{a} \cdot \vec{a}=|\vec{a}|^2 \\ \vec{a} \cdot \vec{b} & =\vec{b} \cdot \vec{a} \quad \text { the commutative law } \\ \vec{a} \cdot(\vec{b}+\vec{c}) & =\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}+\vec{a} \cdot \vec{c} \quad \text { the distributive law } \\ (s \vec{a}) \cdot \vec{b} & =s(\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b})=\vec{a} \cdot(s \vec{b}) \end{aligned} $$

## Angle between 2 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$
The angle between 2 vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ The angle $v$ between two vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ is determined by $$ \cos (v)=\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}||\vec{b}|} $$
## Projection
Suppose $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ are actual vectors. Then the projection is $\overrightarrow{b_a}$ of $\vec{b}$ on $\vec{a}$ $$ \overrightarrow{b_a}=\frac{\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}}{|\vec{a}|^2} \vec{a} $$The length of the projection of $\vec{b}$ on $\vec{a}$ is $$ \left|\overrightarrow{b_a}\right|=\frac{|\vec{a} \cdot \vec{b}|} {|\vec{a}|} $$

## Cross product
![[Pasted image 20230827155031.png]]
The cross product of $$ \vec{a}=\left(\begin{array}{c} a_1 \\ a_2 \\ a_3 \end{array}\right) \text { and } \vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{l} b_1 \\ b_2 \\ b_3 \end{array}\right) $$ has the coordinates $$ \vec{a} \times \vec{b}=(|\vec{a}||\vec{b}| \sin (v)) \vec{e}=\left(\begin{array}{c} a_2 b_3-a_3 b_2 \\ a_3 b_1-a_1 b_3 \\ a_1 b_2-a_2 b_1 \end{array}\right) $$ and can be calculated as follows: $$ \vec{a} \times \vec{b}=\left(\begin{array}{ll} \left|\begin{array}{ll} a_2 &b_2 \\ a_3 &b_3 \end{array}\right| \\ \left|\begin{array}{ll} a_3 &b_3 \\ a_1 &b_1 \end{array}\right| \\ \left|\begin{array}{ll} a_1 &b_1 \\ a_2 &b_2 \end{array}\right| \end{array}\right) $$
If the vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ are parallel, then the angle $v$ between them is either $0^{\circ}$ or $180^{\circ}$, so in both cases $\sin(v)=0$, then $$ \vec{a} \times \vec{b}=\overrightarrow{0} $$ By it we get the following sentence: Two vectors are parallel if and only if $$ \vec{a} \times \vec{b}=\overrightarrow{0} $$ The area of the parallelogram spanned by two actual vectors $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$ can be seen as follows

# Lines in the space
A line in space is determined by either knowing a point on the line and a vector parallel to the line, or knowing two points on the line. A vector parallel to the line we call a direction vector for the line, and since we can find infinitely many vectors parallel to the line, a line has infinitely many direction vectors. We now look at the $l$ line that goes through $P_0$ and has $\vec{r}$ as the direction vector. For any other point $P$ of $l$, $\overrightarrow{P_0 P}$ is parallel to $\vec{r}$, so a real number $t$ must be found, so $$ \overrightarrow{P_0 P}=t \cdot \vec{r} $$
![[Pasted image 20230827164000.png]]
The parameter representation in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

The line $l$ through $P_0\left(x_0, y_0, z_0\right)$ with the direction vector $$ \vec{r}=\left(\begin{array}{l} r_1 \\ r_2 \\ r_3 \end{array}\right) $$ has the parameter representation $$ l:\left(\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \\ z \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} x_0 \\ y_0 \\ z_0 \end{array}\right)+t\left(\begin{array}{l} r_1 \\ r_2 \\ r_3 \end{array}\right), t \in \mathbb{R} $$
# Sphere equation
A sphere in space consists of points that are all at the same distance $r$ from a point $C$. The point $C$ is callled the centre of the sphere, and the number $r$ is the radius of the sphere. If $C$ has the coordinates $(a, b, c)$, then the point $P(x, y, z)$ will be on the sphere, precisely when $|C P|=r$.
![[Pasted image 20230828104103.png]]

A point $\mathrm{P}(\mathrm{x}, \mathrm{y}, \mathrm{z})$ lies on the sphere centred in $\mathrm{C}(\mathrm{a}, \mathrm{b}, \mathrm{c})$ and radius $\mathrm{r}$, if and only if $$ (x-a)^2+(y-b)^2+(z-c)^2=r^2 $$

# Plane equation
A plane $\alpha$ in space is determined by a point $P_0\left(x_0, y_0, z_0\right)$ in the plane and a vector $\vec{n}$ that is orthogonal on the plane. We call the vector $\vec{n}$ a normal vector for the plane.
![[Pasted image 20230828104820.png]]
Let $P_0\left(x_0, y_0, z_0\right)$ be a point in the plane, and let $$ \vec{n}=\left(\begin{array}{l} a \\ b \\ c \end{array}\right) $$ be a normal vector for the plane. A point $P(x, y, z)$ is in the plane if and only if $$ a\left(x-x_0\right)+b\left(y-y_0\right)+c\left(z-z_0\right)=0 $$ or $$ a x+b y+c z+d=0 $$ where $d=-a x_0-b y_0-c z_0$.

## Example (intersection between sphere and a line in the space)
We must find any intersections of line 1 with the parameter representation: $$ \left(\begin{array}{l} x \\ y \\ z \end{array}\right)=\left(\begin{array}{l} 3 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{array}\right)+t\left(\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \\ -1 \end{array}\right) $$ and the sphere with the equation $$ \mathcal{K}:(x-2)^2+(y+1)^2+(z-4)^2=16 $$ We insert the coordinate expressions $$ x=3+t, y=1+2 t \text { and } z=3-t $$ in the equation for the sphere and convert $$ \begin{aligned} & (3+t-2)^2+(1+2 t+1)^2+(3-t-4)^2=16 \\ \Leftrightarrow & (t+1)^2+(2 t+2)^2+(-t-1)^2=16 \\ \Leftrightarrow & t^2+2 t+1+4 t^2+8 t+4+t^2+2 t+1=16 \\ \Leftrightarrow & 6 t^2+12 t-10=0 \\ \Leftrightarrow & 3 t^2+6 t-5=0 \end{aligned} $$ The discriminant is $6^2-4 \cdot 3 \cdot(-5)=96$, so the line intersects the sphere at two points, corresponding to the parameter values $$ T=\frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{96}}{6}=-1 \pm \frac{4 \sqrt{6}}{6}=-1 \pm \frac{2 \sqrt{6}}{3} $$
When inserting into the parameter expression of the line, we get the intersections $$ \left(2+\frac{2 \sqrt{6}}{3},-1+\frac{4 \sqrt{6}}{3}, 4-\frac{2 \sqrt{6}}{3}\right) $$ $\operatorname{and}$ $$ \left(2-\frac{2 \sqrt{6}}{3},-1-\frac{4 \sqrt{6}}{3}, 4+\frac{2 \sqrt{6}}{3}\right) $$
